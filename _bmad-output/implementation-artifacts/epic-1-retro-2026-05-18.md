# Epic 1 Retrospective: Bootstrap Canonical Contract For Consumers And Adapters

## Epic Summary

Epic 1 established the Hexalith.Folders foundation for downstream feature work. The epic moved from scaffold and repository policy into a concrete OpenAPI Contract Spine, idempotency and adapter-parity rules, generated SDK/idempotency helper generation, the C13 parity oracle, and CI gates for contract drift, generated-client consistency, safety invariants, exit criteria, and parity completeness.

Sprint status shows 16 of 16 Epic 1 stories marked `done`. The retrospective status was `optional` before this review.

## What Went Well

- The team successfully turned architecture decisions into executable safeguards. Early scaffold checks evolved into CI gates for contract drift, generated clients, safety invariants, governance completeness, and parity completeness.
- The Contract Spine became the single source of truth for REST, SDK, CLI, MCP, generated helpers, and parity-oracle expectations instead of letting each surface define behavior independently.
- Review discipline was strong. Many stories used party-mode review, advanced elicitation, and adversarial code review to convert vague decisions into specific contract fields, validation tests, and follow-up work.
- Negative scope was preserved. Runtime handlers, provider adapters, workers, UI behavior, CLI/MCP commands, and filesystem/Git side effects were repeatedly held out of Epic 1 unless a story explicitly owned them.
- The zero-leak posture became concrete. Metadata-only audit, redaction, tenant-boundary, cache-key, safety-sentinel, and generated-artifact checks are now expressed as fixtures, tests, or gates.

## Challenges And Growth Areas

- Story boundaries needed repeated correction. Several reviews found scope creep or hidden coupling, especially when later-story artifacts appeared in early scaffold work or Story 2 artifacts drifted into Epic 1 changes.
- Contract vocabulary changes often had cross-story impact. File/context contracts, audit diagnostics, idempotency metadata, parity rows, and extension vocabulary all depended on each other, which made isolated patches harder.
- Review cycles surfaced many edge cases late in the story flow. Examples include redaction-state invariants, stream versus inline transport semantics, safe-denial routing, path policy vocabulary, generated-client hash behavior, and governance diagnostic semantics.
- Deferred work accumulated across stories. Most items are non-blocking hardening tasks, but the list now needs active triage before Epic 2 implementation starts depending on the same surfaces.
- Sprint metadata drift is already visible in Epic 2. Story 2.4 was marked `ready-for-dev` even though its artifact had been intentionally removed, and Story 2.1 is now in progress.

## Key Lessons

1. Contract-first work still needs executable boundaries. Prose alone was not enough; the successful stories added schema assertions, local scripts, and CI workflows that make drift visible.
2. Negative scope should be treated as a deliverable. The best story records explicitly said what was not implemented and which future story owned it.
3. Safety rules need contaminated negative controls and sanitized diagnostics. The team improved once it tested failure behavior without echoing forbidden values.
4. Adapter parity has two layers: transport parity can collapse around SDK-vs-REST, but CLI/MCP behavioral parity still needs explicit rules for pre-SDK errors, failure-kind projection, exit codes, and side-channel parameters.
5. Story metadata is part of the system. Status-artifact drift can block pre-development flows just as effectively as a failing test.

## Next Epic Preview

Epic 2 is `Tenant-Scoped Folder Access And Lifecycle`. It moves from contract scaffolding into tenant-aware domain behavior: Tenants event integration, organization ACL baseline, folder creation, access grants and revocation, effective-permission inspection, layered safe denials, lifecycle/binding status, archival, and worker reactions to Tenants events.

Epic 2 depends directly on Epic 1's decisions:

- Authorization must evaluate tenant projection and folder ACL before resource access.
- Events and diagnostics must remain metadata-only.
- Folder IDs, organization IDs, task IDs, operation IDs, idempotency keys, and correlation IDs must preserve the canonical shapes from the Contract Spine.
- Runtime behavior must respect the safety and governance gates rather than bypassing them with implementation-only shortcuts.
- CLI, MCP, SDK, and REST behavior must remain traceable to the same contract and parity dimensions.

## Significant Discoveries

- Epic 1 revealed that generated artifacts are not passive outputs; they need their own drift and safety checks.
- OpenAPI 3.1 composition and strict validation require careful schema design, especially around `allOf`, `oneOf`, redaction variants, and conditional fields.
- Safe-denial behavior cannot be inferred per endpoint. It needs explicit categories, audience handling, and metadata boundaries.
- The C13 parity oracle and governance gates are now central infrastructure for later implementation stories, not optional documentation helpers.
- Story 2.4 readiness drift shows that Epic 2 planning metadata needs cleanup before additional stories are started.
